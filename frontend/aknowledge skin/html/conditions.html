Common Skin Conditions

Learn about various skin conditions, their causes, symptoms, and treatment options.

Understanding Skin Conditions

Skin conditions can affect anyone at any age. They can be temporary or permanent, and may be painless or cause discomfort. Some conditions are situational and appear in response to triggers, while others are genetic.

Early diagnosis and treatment can help manage symptoms and prevent complications. If you're experiencing persistent skin issues, consult with a dermatologist for proper evaluation.

Acne is a common skin condition that occurs when hair follicles become clogged with oil and dead skin cells, leading to whiteheads, blackheads, or pimples.

Causes

  • Excess oil production
  • Hair follicles clogged by oil and dead skin cells
  • Bacteria (Propionibacterium acnes)
  • Inflammation
  • Hormonal changes
  • Certain medications
  • Diet (high-glycemic foods, dairy)
  • Stress

Symptoms

  • Whiteheads (closed plugged pores)
  • Blackheads (open plugged pores)
  • Papules (small red, tender bumps)
  • Pustules (papules with pus at their tips)
  • Nodules (large, solid, painful lumps beneath the skin)
  • Cystic lesions (painful, pus-filled lumps beneath the skin)

Treatment Options

  • Topical treatments (benzoyl peroxide, salicylic acid, retinoids)
  • Oral medications (antibiotics, combined oral contraceptives)
  • Isotretinoin for severe cases
  • Chemical peels
  • Light therapy
  • Extraction of large cysts

Eczema is a chronic condition that causes the skin to become itchy, red, dry, and cracked. It's common in children but can occur at any age.

Causes

  • Genetic factors
  • Immune system dysfunction
  • Environmental triggers
  • Skin barrier dysfunction
  • Allergies
  • Stress

Symptoms

  • Dry, sensitive skin
  • Intense itching
  • Red to brownish-gray patches
  • Small, raised bumps
  • Thickened, cracked, scaly skin
  • Raw, sensitive skin from scratching

Treatment Options

  • Moisturizers to prevent dryness
  • Topical corticosteroids to reduce inflammation
  • Topical calcineurin inhibitors
  • Antihistamines for itching
  • Phototherapy
  • Immunosuppressants for severe cases
  • Biologics (dupilumab)

Psoriasis is a chronic autoimmune condition that causes rapid buildup of skin cells, resulting in scaling on the skin's surface.

Causes

  • Immune system dysfunction
  • Genetic predisposition
  • Environmental triggers
  • Stress
  • Infections (strep throat)
  • Injuries to the skin
  • Certain medications

Symptoms

  • Red patches of skin covered with silvery scales
  • Dry, cracked skin that may bleed
  • Itching, burning, or soreness
  • Thickened, pitted, or ridged nails
  • Swollen and stiff joints

Treatment Options

  • Topical treatments (corticosteroids, vitamin D analogues)
  • Light therapy (phototherapy)
  • Oral medications (methotrexate, cyclosporine)
  • Biologics (targeting specific immune system pathways)
  • Lifestyle modifications

Rosacea is a chronic inflammatory skin condition that primarily affects the face, causing redness, visible blood vessels, and sometimes small, red, pus-filled bumps.

Causes

  • Blood vessel abnormalities
  • Demodex folliculorum mites
  • Genetic factors
  • Immune system reactions
  • Environmental factors

Symptoms

  • Facial redness (persistent)
  • Visible blood vessels (telangiectasia)
  • Swollen red bumps (papules and pustules)
  • Eye problems (dry, irritated, swollen eyes)
  • Enlarged nose (rhinophyma)
  • Burning or stinging sensation

Treatment Options

  • Topical medications (metronidazole, azelaic acid)
  • Oral antibiotics (doxycycline)
  • Isotretinoin for severe cases
  • Laser therapy for visible blood vessels
  • Trigger avoidance
  • Gentle skincare routine

Melasma is a common skin condition that causes brown or grayish-brown patches on the face, particularly on the cheeks, bridge of the nose, forehead, chin, and above the upper lip.

Causes

  • Hormonal changes (pregnancy, birth control pills)
  • Sun exposure
  • Genetic predisposition
  • Skin care products that irritate the skin

Symptoms

  • Brown or gray-brown patches
  • Symmetrical patches on both sides of the face
  • Most common on the cheeks, forehead, nose, and upper lip
  • No other symptoms (not painful or itchy)

Treatment Options

  • Sun protection (most important)
  • Topical treatments (hydroquinone, tretinoin, corticosteroids)
  • Chemical peels
  • Microdermabrasion
  • Laser treatments
  • Hormone regulation
